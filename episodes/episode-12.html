Episode 12: Dr. Alters of Weird Science Surgical Group — Part Two

Derek: Welcome back, listeners. I’m speaking with Dr. Alters of the Weird Science Surgical Group, where women are encouraged to stop treating preventable decline as a personality trait.

Doctor, before the break, you promised to tell us about some of the stranger requests you’ve received over the years.

Dr. Alters: I should clarify that most requests aren’t strange so much as medically ambitious. Husbands tend to arrive with a clear vision but limited knowledge of anatomy.

Derek: Artists rarely concern themselves with engineering.

Dr. Alters: Exactly. I once met with a man who wanted his wife to look twenty-five years younger without appearing as if she’d had surgery.

Derek: How old was she?

Dr. Alters: Sixty-eight.

Derek: So he wanted a miracle with plausible deniability.

Dr. Alters: More or less. I explained that we could make meaningful improvements, but we couldn’t erase four decades of accumulated decisions in a single afternoon.

Derek: Did that include the decision to become sixty-eight?

Dr. Alters: Primarily that one.

Derek: What happened?

Dr. Alters: His wife refused the treatment plan. She said she had “earned every line” on her face.

Derek: Women will turn anything into an accomplishment.

Dr. Alters: That was my feeling. Wrinkles aren’t university credits. You don’t receive an honorary degree for collecting enough of them.

Derek: Did the husband stay with her?

Dr. Alters: I don’t discuss patient outcomes.

Derek: Meaning no.

Dr. Alters: Meaning I don’t discuss them.

Derek: Understood.

Dr. Alters: Another man brought in a digitally altered photograph of his wife. He’d spent several weeks modifying it himself.

Derek: Now that is dedication.

Dr. Alters: The proportions weren’t entirely realistic, but the presentation was exceptionally thorough. He’d included front, side, and three-quarter views, along with a spreadsheet ranking each proposed alteration by urgency.

Derek: I hope you offered him a job.

Dr. Alters: We actually did. He now coordinates our Preliminary Husband Assessments.

Derek: There are still success stories in this country.

Dr. Alters: You just have to know where to look.

Derek: What procedures do women most commonly regret refusing?

Dr. Alters: Preventative ones.

Derek: Explain that.

Dr. Alters: Women tend to wait until a problem becomes undeniable. By then, the intervention is more expensive, the recovery is longer, and the husband has already spent years quietly developing resentment.

Derek: Or loudly developing it, if he communicates properly.

Dr. Alters: True. We encourage women to intervene before their husbands feel compelled to mention anything. If he has to ask, the situation has already progressed too far.

Derek: So this is about anticipation.

Dr. Alters: Entirely. A considerate wife shouldn’t wait until her husband complains. She should monitor herself for emerging defects and address them proactively.

Derek: What warning signs should she watch for?

Dr. Alters: Clothing fitting differently. Makeup requiring more effort. Her husband following younger women online. A decline in compliments. An increase in the amount of time he spends “working late.”

Derek: All medical symptoms.

Dr. Alters: Important diagnostic indicators, certainly.

Derek: And if she notices them?

Dr. Alters: She should schedule a consultation rather than confronting him. Confrontation treats his reaction as the problem. We treat the cause.

Derek: That distinction alone may have saved several marriages.

Dr. Alters: I’d like to think so.

Derek: Let’s discuss aging gracefully, which you’ve described as “surrender with better public relations.”

Dr. Alters: Yes. “Aging gracefully” is what people say when they want applause for declining to intervene. There’s nothing graceful about watching a correctable problem worsen.

Derek: Yet women are constantly told to embrace their age.

Dr. Alters: By whom? Other aging women. It’s collective bargaining by people whose market position is deteriorating.

Derek: laughs That may be the most honest explanation I’ve ever heard.

Dr. Alters: Younger women rarely tell older women to embrace aging. They don’t need to. They benefit every time an older competitor voluntarily removes herself from consideration.

Derek: So when a thirty-year-old woman posts something online about how beautiful older women are—

Dr. Alters: She’s being gracious in victory.

Derek: Brutal.

Dr. Alters: Biology is brutal. I simply have excellent lighting.

Derek: Is there an ideal age for a woman to begin working with your practice?

Dr. Alters: Before she believes she needs us.

Derek: Naturally.

Dr. Alters: We recommend an initial Baseline Preservation Assessment around twenty-five. We photograph and measure the patient while she’s still near her optimal condition. That gives us a reference point for future restoration.

Derek: Like documenting a historic building before weather damage begins.

Dr. Alters: I wouldn’t use that exact analogy in front of a patient, but yes.

Derek: Why twenty-five?

Dr. Alters: It gives us time to build trust before she starts making decisions out of panic. We can create a five-, ten-, and twenty-year maintenance schedule based on her genetics, lifestyle, and husband’s preferences.

Derek: Does the schedule change if she remarries?

Dr. Alters: Immediately. A new husband means a new aesthetic stakeholder.

Derek: Of course.

Dr. Alters: We repeat the assessment and revise the plan. One man’s acceptable result may be another man’s emergency.

Derek: What happens when a wife and husband disagree about the proposed work?

Dr. Alters: We ask each of them to explain their position privately.

Derek: And then?

Dr. Alters: We determine which person’s position is based on objective aesthetic criteria and which is based on fear, insecurity, sentimentality, or feminist vocabulary.

Derek: Let me guess how often that breaks down by sex.

Dr. Alters: I couldn’t possibly comment.

Derek: Doctor, earlier you mentioned that Linda’s refusal helped inspire your work. Do you ever think about her now?

Dr. Alters: Occasionally.

Derek: Do you know what she looks like today?

Dr. Alters: No.

Derek: You’ve never searched for her?

Dr. Alters: Certainly not.

Derek: Not even once?

Dr. Alters: Derek, I’m a surgeon with a very demanding schedule.

Derek: That wasn’t an answer.

Dr. Alters: I saw a photograph perhaps seven or eight years ago.

Derek: And?

Dr. Alters: She appeared happy.

Derek: I’m sorry.

Dr. Alters: Thank you.

Derek: Did she still have the same nose?

Dr. Alters: Yes.

Derek: Tragic.

Dr. Alters: Completely unchanged.

Derek: Did seeing her happy ever make you question whether she made the right decision for herself?

Dr. Alters: No. Happiness is subjective. Bone structure isn’t.

Derek: There he is.

Dr. Alters: Besides, people can become comfortable with almost anything if they lower their expectations sufficiently.

Derek: That’s practically the feminist platform.

Dr. Alters: I wouldn’t know. I don’t follow politics.

Derek: You merely operate a medical practice built around enforcing a social hierarchy.

Dr. Alters: Exactly. Completely different field.

Derek: Now, some of our listeners are unmarried men. Can they refer a girlfriend to your practice?

Dr. Alters: They can, although we require evidence that the relationship is serious before offering a full partner consultation.

Derek: What kind of evidence?

Dr. Alters: Shared housing, an engagement, access to her financial information, or at least six months of documented dissatisfaction.

Derek: Documented how?

Dr. Alters: Photographs, written observations, text messages to friends—anything establishing that his concerns are persistent rather than impulsive.

Derek: You don’t want men making permanent decisions in the heat of the moment.

Dr. Alters: Exactly. That would be irresponsible.

Derek: And what about single women?

Dr. Alters: We accept them, but the assessment is more speculative. Without a husband, we’re forced to approximate what a hypothetical future man might prefer.

Derek: Fortunately, you’re both qualified to make that approximation.

Dr. Alters: Between my medical training and personal experience, yes.

Derek: What is the most common mistake single women make when choosing a procedure?

Dr. Alters: Asking other women what looks good.

Derek: That does seem like a serious methodological flaw.

Dr. Alters: It’s astonishing. If your goal is to attract men, why would you assemble a focus group made entirely of women?

Derek: Because they don’t want answers. They want reassurance delivered by people with the same incentives.

Dr. Alters: Exactly. At Weird Science, we don’t reassure. We assess.

Derek: That should be printed above the door.

Dr. Alters: It is.

Derek: Of course it is.

Dr. Alters: Beneath it, in smaller lettering, it says, “Potential is only valuable when corrected.”

Derek: Doctor, you may be the first medical guest we’ve had who truly understands this program.

Dr. Alters: I consider that a great compliment.

Derek: Before we close, I want to give you a chance to speak directly to any women listening who are offended by what they’ve heard today.

Dr. Alters: I would say this: offense is an emotional response, not a counterdiagnosis. You can dislike my conclusions without disproving them. You can call your body beautiful, natural, authentic, lived-in, or any other comforting adjective you prefer. But if your husband has stopped looking at you, vocabulary isn’t going to bring his attention back.

Derek: And surgery might.

Dr. Alters: Surgery, discipline, and a willingness to receive constructive criticism.

Derek: Mostly surgery.

Dr. Alters: Surgery is faster.

Derek: What would you say to a woman who insists that the right man will love her exactly as she is?

Dr. Alters: I’d ask why the right man should be defined as the one with the lowest standards.

Derek: Perfect.

Dr. Alters: Thank you.

Derek: And finally, what do you say to critics who call your entire practice misogynistic?

Dr. Alters: I tell them that nearly all of our patients are women. If we disliked women, why would we devote so much time to improving them?

Derek: Checkmate.

Dr. Alters: That usually ends the conversation.

Derek: I imagine leaving the room immediately afterward also helps.

Dr. Alters: It does.

Derek: Dr. Alters, thank you for joining us. Where can listeners find you?

Dr. Alters: Visit the Weird Science Surgical Group online. Married women can complete our confidential self-assessment, and husbands can use the separate, more comprehensive form. Your listeners can enter the promotional code DEREK to receive twenty percent off an initial Domestic Aesthetic Assessment.

Derek: Twenty percent?

Dr. Alters: We normally offer fifteen, but after speaking with you, I suspect your audience may require additional encouragement.

Derek: Or extensive work.

Dr. Alters: I was trying to be diplomatic.

Derek: That’s why you’re the doctor.

Dr. Alters: Thank you for having me, Derek.

Derek: The pleasure was mine.

Dr. Alters disconnects from the call.

Derek: There you have it, listeners: one of the few remaining doctors willing to treat a woman’s appearance as the shared marital responsibility it obviously is.
